Palette Direction — three candidates, not three tunings

You called the current palette pale and vanilla. That's a direction problem, not a saturation-slider problem. Before we touch Colors.ts or styles.css, here are three genuinely different personas to choose between. Pick the lane first, then we tune.

Ground rules I am holding: WCAG AA on body copy stays non-negotiable (we do not weaken the 16:1 onyx-on-stone we have today); this is a sacred space, not a fintech app, so "vibrant" here means richer pigment and tonal confidence — not Pantone-of-the-year or Muslim-Pro neon. Every jewel tone below references an actual Islamic material tradition (zellige, manuscript illumination, tile, gilt) rather than a trending gradient.

Candidate A — Deep & Cinematic evening prayer

Sapphire stops being a tint and becomes a surface. Hero moments — splash, prayer countdown, announcement detail — sit on a deep midnight-navy canvas with a warmer, richer gold as the single ornamental move. Stone becomes the secondary mode, not primary. The posture is Linear's dark-first elegance crossed with the lit interior of Süleymaniye at isha. References: Linear's marketing surfaces, Apple Weather's nighttime palette, the Athan Pro dark theme done with twice the confidence.

Gain: immediate drama, photographic weight, gold actually glints. Feels like a place, not a list. Lose: light mode becomes the "secondary" experience, which breaks older congregants' default expectation; battery/OLED implications are a plus but UX decision must be deliberate.

Candidate B — Editorial Jewel illuminated page

Cream canvas stays, but everything on top of it gets weight. Deeper indigo replaces the current sapphire, a richer burnished gold replaces the muted tan, and we add one restrained jewel accent — a deep zellige emerald — used sparingly for success, live-now states, and one editorial moment per screen. Think The New York Times magazine spread meets an Ottoman manuscript frontispiece. References: Quran.com's restrained confidence, Teenage Engineering's tonal discipline, Rauno Freiberg's "structural color" approach where one saturated hue does all the heavy lifting.

Gain: preserves the light-first sanctuary feel while pushing contrast up hard; emerald introduces tradition without neon; closest to "scholarly / not designery". Lose: risks looking like the current palette unless stone tiers deepen meaningfully — requires courage on stone-300/400.

Candidate C — Gilded Warm illuminated manuscript

The stones themselves deepen into parchment and terracotta territory, and gold expands from accent to occasional surface (section headers, hero card tops, the prayer-active bar). This is the warmest candidate — closer to the feel of a worn mus-haf cover than to a screen. References: the Alhambra's ochre interior light, the Topkapı manuscripts wing, and — the outlier — Hallow's deeply confident warm direction, which is the one religious app that broke its category and did not look like everyone else in the store.

Gain: unmistakable identity, warmth that survives screenshots, gold earns the "divine" label. Lose: least versatile for dense content (schedules, event lists) — can read as decorative; hardest to keep readable at small type sizes.

Recommendation — Candidate A (Deep & Cinematic)

The screenshots that triggered your "pale / vanilla" note were almost all light mode hero surfaces where the canvas dominates and sapphire only appears as a small tint. That is a surface hierarchy problem, not a hex problem. A is the only candidate that fixes it at the root, by letting sapphire own the hero. B is the safer follow-up if A feels too much of a pivot — it reaches the same weight without moving off the cream canvas. C I would hold in reserve for a Ramadan Mode variant rather than the default theme.
